The politics of foreign aid and how it's spent

By KRISTI WALKER

APRIL 5, 2015


The U.S. is thought of as one of the most generous countries in the world. In fact, the American public believes that 25% of its budget is spent on foreign aid, even though the actual amount hardly scrapes the surface. Politics likely have a heavy influence on funding, and while the country still provides trillions of dollars in assistance, the aid is often disproportionate among its benefiters. The U.S. Agency for International Development, which is responsible for foreign assistance, received an extra $7 billion from the government in 2007, the year that democrats won control of congress from a previously red house and senate. Throughout most of the blue reign, funding continued to rise or stay the same. However, USAID saw a decrease in funding of $8 billion when republicans took back congress this year.

While USAID provided assistance to 126 countries in 2014, it tended to focus on Middle Eastern and African countries. Among those, about 9 percent of the $11 trillion in disbursements went to Afghanistan, which received $10 billion more in aid than Jordan, the second most funded country. Of the disbursement categories, USAID spent the most on assisting with peace and security, economic development, and health, while issues like the environment received less attention.
